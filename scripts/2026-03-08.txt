Parashah: Vayakhel-Pekudei (Exodus 35:1-40:38)
Date: 2026-03-08

Welcome back to Parashah in Brief! I'm so glad you're joining me this week. We've arrived at Vayakhel-Pekudei, a powerful double portion that brings the book of Exodus to its grand conclusion — and it's all about the Israelites coming together to build a sacred home for God's presence.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Exodus, chapter thirty-five, verse one, through chapter forty, verse thirty-eight. Moses gathers the entire Israelite community and begins with a reminder: six days you may work, but the seventh day — the Sabbath — is holy, a day of complete rest. Then he turns to something extraordinary. He asks the people to bring freewill offerings for the construction of the Mishkan, the Tabernacle — a portable sanctuary where God will dwell among them. And the response is breathtaking. Men and women alike come forward, their hearts stirred, bringing gold, silver, copper, fine linens, yarns dyed blue and purple and crimson, precious stones, oils, and spices. The skilled women spin yarn and goats' hair with their own hands. The chieftains contribute lapis lazuli and other rare materials. The outpouring is so overwhelming that the craftsmen actually come to Moses and say, "The people are bringing more than we need!" — and Moses has to send a proclamation through the camp telling everyone to stop giving. Can you imagine that? Then Moses introduces the master artisans God has chosen to lead the work: Bezalel of the tribe of Judah, filled with divine skill in every kind of craft, and Oholiab of the tribe of Dan, a gifted carver, designer, and embroiderer. Together with all the skilled workers, they set about constructing the Tabernacle in exquisite detail — the golden Ark with its two cherubim whose wings stretch out protectively over the cover, the golden lampstand hammered from a single piece of pure gold, the incense altar, the table for the bread of display, and the great bronze altar for burnt offerings. They weave the magnificent curtains with their cherubim designs, build the wooden planks and bars for the walls, and erect the entire courtyard enclosure. Even the copper laver is fashioned from the mirrors donated by the women who served at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. Every detail Moses received on Mount Sinai is now faithfully brought to life by the hands and hearts of the people.

Parashah: Emor (Leviticus 21:1-24:23)
Date: 2026-04-26

Welcome back to Parashah in Brief, so glad you're joining me this week. This week's Torah portion is called Emor, which means "speak," and it's found in the book of Leviticus, continuing the detailed laws God gives to Moses at Mount Sinai for how the Israelite community is to live as a holy people.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Leviticus, chapter twenty-one, verse one, through chapter twenty-four, verse twenty-three. The portion opens with God telling Moses to instruct the priests — the sons of Aaron — about special standards of holiness they must uphold. Ordinary priests may not come into contact with the dead except for their closest relatives, and they must not shave their heads or cut their flesh in mourning. The High Priest, the one anointed with oil and clothed in sacred vestments, is held to an even stricter standard — he may not defile himself for any dead person at all, not even his own parents, and he may only marry a virgin. God also specifies that any priest who has a physical defect may eat the sacred food but may not approach the altar to make offerings. The portion then turns to the sacred donations themselves, laying out who may eat of them and under what conditions of purity. Next, God instructs that any animal brought as an offering must be without blemish — nothing blind, injured, or maimed will be accepted. Then comes one of the great centerpieces of this portion: the calendar of sacred times. God lays out the weekly Sabbath, the Passover and Feast of Unleavened Bread in spring, the counting of fifty days to the festival of first fruits, the day of loud blasts on the first of the seventh month, the solemn Day of Atonement on the tenth, and the joyful Feast of Booths on the fifteenth — when the people are to dwell in temporary shelters to remember that God brought them out of Egypt. The portion also includes instructions for the eternal lamp and the twelve loaves of bread set before God each Sabbath. Finally, a dramatic story unfolds: a man of mixed Israelite and Egyptian parentage blasphemes God's name during a fight, and God decrees that the whole community shall stone him, establishing the principle of one law for stranger and citizen alike.

Parashah: Shmini (Leviticus 9:1-11:47)
Date: 2026-04-05

Welcome to Parashah in Brief, and thanks so much for joining me this week. We're looking at Parashat Shmini, which means "eighth" — as in the eighth day. This portion picks up right where the consecration of the Tabernacle left off, as Israel's priests prepare to serve God for the very first time.

Here is our summary of Leviticus, chapter nine, verse one, through chapter eleven, verse forty-seven. The portion opens on the eighth day after the seven days of priestly ordination. Moses calls Aaron and his sons and instructs them to bring a series of offerings — purgation offerings, burnt offerings, grain offerings, and offerings of well-being — because God has promised to appear before the people that very day. Aaron steps up to the altar and carries out each sacrifice with care and precision, first for himself and then on behalf of all Israel. When he finishes, Aaron lifts his hands and blesses the people, and then he and Moses enter the Tent of Meeting together. When they come out, the Presence of God appears to the entire community, and a divine fire leaps forth and consumes the offerings on the altar. The people cry out in awe and fall on their faces — it is a breathtaking, triumphant moment. But then, almost immediately, tragedy strikes. Aaron's two eldest sons, Nadab and Abihu, each take a fire pan and offer what the text calls "alien fire," something God had not commanded. Fire comes forth from God again — but this time it consumes them, and they die. Moses turns to Aaron and says that God has declared, "Through those near to Me I show Myself holy." And Aaron, in one of the most powerful moments in all of Torah, is silent. Moses then instructs the remaining family on how to grieve and how to carry on with their sacred duties. God speaks directly to Aaron, commanding the priests never to drink wine before entering the Tent of Meeting, so they can distinguish between the sacred and the ordinary. The portion then shifts to the dietary laws, laying out in careful detail which animals, fish, birds, and insects the Israelites may and may not eat — teaching the people that holiness extends into the most everyday act of sitting down to a meal. God's closing charge is both simple and profound: "You shall be holy, for I am holy."

Parashah: Pesach Shabbat Chol haMoed (Exodus 33:12-34:26)
Date: 2026-03-29

Welcome to Parashah in Brief, and a happy Passover to all of you. This week, during the intermediate Shabbat of Pesach — the festival of Passover — we read a special Torah portion drawn from the book of Exodus. It takes us to a deeply intimate moment between Moses and God, set against the backdrop of crisis and renewal after the sin of the golden calf.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Exodus, chapter thirty-three, verse twelve, through chapter thirty-four, verse twenty-six. The portion opens with Moses pressing God in conversation. He says, essentially, "You've told me to lead this people, but I need to know — are You truly going with us?" Moses pleads that without God's presence, the people of Israel have no way to be distinguished among the nations of the earth. God reassures him, saying, "I will go with you, and I will lighten your burden." Then Moses makes an extraordinary request — he asks to see God's very presence, God's glory. God responds with profound tenderness and mystery: "I will make all My goodness pass before you, but you cannot see My face, for no mortal can see Me and live." Instead, God places Moses in a cleft in the rock, shields him as the divine presence passes by, and allows Moses to glimpse only God's back. Next, God commands Moses to carve two new stone tablets to replace the ones he shattered after the golden calf. Moses rises early, climbs Mount Sinai alone, and there God descends in a cloud and proclaims what are known as the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy — declaring, "God, a deity compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in kindness and faithfulness, forgiving iniquity, transgression, and sin." Moses bows immediately and begs God to remain among the people, even though they are stiff-necked and prone to failure. God then declares a covenant, promising wonders unlike anything the world has seen, and warning the people against idolatry and entanglements with the nations of the land. The portion closes with a series of sacred instructions: observe the Feast of Unleavened Bread, consecrate the firstborn, rest on the seventh day, and celebrate the three pilgrimage festivals — including, fittingly, Passover itself.

Parashah: Achrei Mot-Kedoshim (Leviticus 16:1-20:27)
Date: 2026-04-19

Welcome back to Parashah in Brief, the podcast where we walk through the weekly Torah portion together. This week we're reading a double portion called Achrei Mot-Kedoshim, which picks up right after the tragic death of Aaron's two sons and carries us deep into the heart of what it means to live as a holy community.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Leviticus, chapter sixteen, verse one, through chapter twenty, verse twenty-seven. The portion opens in the shadow of loss. Aaron's two sons have just died after approaching God's presence in the wrong way, and now God tells Moses to warn Aaron: he must not enter the innermost holy space, the area behind the curtain where the Ark rests, whenever he pleases — or he too will die. Instead, God lays out an elaborate ritual for one special day each year, what will become Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement. Aaron must bathe, dress in simple white linen garments, and bring specific offerings — a bull and two goats. He casts lots over the two goats: one is sacrificed to God, and the other, the goat for Azazel, has all the sins of the people symbolically placed upon its head before being sent away into the wilderness. Through clouds of incense and the careful sprinkling of blood, the innermost shrine, the tent, and the altar are all purged of the people's impurities. It is a solemn reset, commanded as a permanent practice for all generations. The portion then shifts, forbidding the Israelites from consuming blood, declaring that the life force of all creatures resides in the blood. Chapter eighteen warns against the practices of Egypt and Canaan, listing forbidden sexual relationships as boundaries the community must honor. Then comes the soaring call of chapter nineteen: "You shall be holy, for I, the Eternal your God, am holy." What follows is a breathtaking cascade of ethical laws — revere your parents, keep the Sabbath, leave the corners of your fields unharvested for the poor and the stranger, pay workers on time, do not insult the deaf or place a stumbling block before the blind, judge fairly, and do not bear a grudge. At the heart of it all stands one of the Torah's most famous teachings: love your fellow as yourself. The portion closes by reminding Israel that holiness is not only ritual — it is woven into every relationship, every transaction, every act of compassion.

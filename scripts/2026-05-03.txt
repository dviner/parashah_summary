Parashah: Behar-Bechukotai (Leviticus 25:1-27:34)
Date: 2026-05-03

Welcome back to Parashah in Brief! I'm glad you're here. This week we have a double portion called Behar-Bechukotai, which brings us to the very end of the book of Leviticus — the third book of the Torah. These final chapters close out a long stretch of laws and instructions given to the Israelites at Mount Sinai.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Leviticus, chapter twenty-five, verse one, through chapter twenty-seven, verse thirty-four. God speaks to Moses on Mount Sinai and introduces a remarkable idea: just as people rest on the seventh day, the land itself must rest every seventh year. For six years the Israelites may sow and harvest, but in the seventh year — called the sabbatical year — the land must lie fallow, and whatever grows on its own is free for everyone to eat. Then, after seven cycles of seven years — forty-nine years in total — a ram's horn is sounded on the Day of Atonement to announce the fiftieth year, known as the Jubilee. In that year, all ancestral land returns to its original family, and anyone who has fallen into servitude among their fellow Israelites goes free. God reminds the people that the land ultimately belongs to God — they are, in a sense, tenants — and they must not take advantage of one another in buying or selling property. Detailed laws follow about redeeming land that has been sold, protecting those who fall into poverty, and prohibiting the charging of interest to a fellow Israelite in need. Then in the second half of the portion, God lays out a powerful choice. If the people follow God's laws, abundant rain will fall, harvests will overflow, peace will reign, and God's presence will dwell among them. But if they turn away and break the covenant, the consequences escalate in waves — disease, drought, wild beasts, siege, exile, and a land left so desolate it finally gets the rest it was denied. Yet even in the darkest moment, God promises never to destroy the people entirely, remembering the covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. The portion closes with laws about vows and consecrations — how to calculate the value when someone dedicates a person, an animal, a house, or a piece of land to God — rules that anchor even our most spontaneous spiritual impulses in fairness and order.

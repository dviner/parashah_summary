Parashah: Bamidbar (Numbers 1:1-4:20)
Date: 2026-05-10

Welcome to Parashah in Brief — so glad you're here with us this week. This week we begin a brand-new book of the Torah as we open Parashat Bamidbar, which means "In the Wilderness," and it launches the fourth of the five books of Moses, picking up the story of the Israelites as they prepare to journey from Mount Sinai toward the Promised Land.

Here is our summary of Numbers, chapter one, verse one, through chapter four, verse twenty. It is now the second year after the Israelites left Egypt, and God speaks to Moses inside the Tent of Meeting — the portable sanctuary at the heart of the camp — with a sweeping command: count the people. Moses and Aaron, assisted by a chieftain from each tribe, conduct a census of every male aged twenty and older who is able to bear arms, and the grand total comes to 603,550 men. But one tribe is deliberately left out of this military count: the Levites. God sets them apart for a sacred purpose — they are to care for the Tabernacle, carry it when the people march, set it up when they stop, and guard it so that no unauthorized person comes near. Next, God lays out a magnificent camp formation. The twelve tribes are arranged in four divisions around the Tabernacle — Judah, Issachar, and Zebulun on the east; Reuben, Simeon, and Gad on the south; Ephraim, Manasseh, and Benjamin on the west; and Dan, Asher, and Naphtali on the north. The Tabernacle and the Levites sit right at the center, the sacred heart of this moving community. We then learn of Aaron's priestly line — his four sons, of whom Nadab and Abihu have already died after offering unauthorized fire before God, leaving Eleazar and Ithamar to serve. God declares that the Levites are taken in place of every firstborn Israelite, a consecration reaching back to the night God struck down the firstborn in Egypt. Finally, the three Levite clans — the families of Gershon, Kohath, and Merari — each receive specific duties: Gershon tends the tent coverings and curtains, Kohath carries the most sacred objects including the Ark, and Merari is responsible for the structural framework, the planks, bars, and posts.

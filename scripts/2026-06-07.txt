Parashah: Sh'lach (Numbers 13:1-15:41)
Date: 2026-06-07

Welcome back to Parashah in Brief, so glad you're here. This week's portion is called Sh'lach, and it picks up with the Israelites camped in the wilderness, poised at the edge of the Promised Land and preparing to see it for the very first time.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Numbers, chapter thirteen, verse one, through chapter fifteen, verse forty-one. God tells Moses to send twelve scouts — one leader from each tribe — into the land of Canaan to survey it. Moses sends them off with clear instructions: check out the people, the cities, the soil, and bring back some fruit. The scouts explore the land for forty days, and they return carrying a cluster of grapes so enormous it takes two men to carry it on a pole, along with pomegranates and figs. They report that the land does indeed flow with milk and honey — but then fear takes over. Ten of the twelve scouts say the inhabitants are giants, the cities are heavily fortified, and the Israelites are like grasshoppers by comparison. Only two scouts, Caleb and Joshua, push back, pleading with the people not to be afraid, insisting that God is with them and they can succeed. But the people don't listen. They weep, they rage against Moses and Aaron, and they even talk about choosing a new leader and heading back to Egypt. God is furious and threatens to destroy the entire nation, but Moses intercedes with a powerful prayer, appealing to God's patience and mercy. God pardons the people, but the consequence is severe: that entire adult generation — everyone twenty years and older who lost faith — will wander the wilderness for forty years and never enter the Promised Land. Only Caleb and Joshua will make it in. The portion then closes with laws about offerings, a sobering story about Sabbath violation, and finally the beautiful commandment to wear fringes, called tzitzit, on the corners of one's garments — a daily, tangible reminder to follow God's commandments and not be led astray by one's own eyes and heart.

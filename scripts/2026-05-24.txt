Parashah: Nasso (Numbers 4:21-7:89)
Date: 2026-05-24

Welcome to Parashah in Brief! I'm so glad you're joining me this week. Today we're looking at Parashat Nasso, which is found in the book of Numbers and is actually the longest single Torah portion in the entire cycle. It picks up right where we left off, with the Israelites camped in the wilderness, organizing their community around the sacred Tabernacle — the portable sanctuary at the center of their camp.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Numbers, chapter four, verse twenty-one, through chapter seven, verse eighty-nine. The portion opens with God instructing Moses to complete the census of the Levites — the tribe dedicated to sacred service — assigning specific duties to the clans of Gershon, Kohath, and Merari. Each clan is responsible for carrying different parts of the Tabernacle as the people travel: the coverings, the wooden frames, the posts, and the sacred objects. In total, over eight thousand Levites between the ages of thirty and fifty are counted for this work. God then commands that anyone in a state of ritual impurity must be sent outside the camp, so that the camp where God dwells remains holy. Next comes a law about wrongdoing between people — when someone wrongs another, they must confess, make full restitution, and add a fifth of the value on top. The portion then describes the dramatic and mysterious ritual of the sotah, or the woman suspected of unfaithfulness by a jealous husband, in which a priest administers bitter waters to determine her innocence or guilt. Following that, we learn about the Nazirite vow — a voluntary commitment by any man or woman to draw closer to God by abstaining from wine, not cutting their hair, and avoiding contact with the dead. When the vow is complete, they bring special offerings and shave their consecrated hair at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. Then comes one of the most beloved passages in all of Torah: the Priestly Blessing, in which Aaron and his sons are taught to bless the people with those luminous words — "May God bless you and protect you, deal kindly and graciously with you, and grant you peace." Finally, the portion concludes with an elaborate twelve-day dedication of the altar, as each tribal chieftain brings identical, generous offerings — silver bowls, gold ladles, animals, and incense — one tribe per day, each honored equally.

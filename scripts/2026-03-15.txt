Parashah: Vayikra (Leviticus 1:1-5:26)
Date: 2026-03-15

Welcome back to Parashah in Brief! I'm so glad you're joining me this week. We are beginning the book of Leviticus, the third book of the Torah, with the portion called Vayikra, which simply means "and He called." After the dramatic story of the Exodus from Egypt and the construction of the Tabernacle, God now turns to the practical details of how the Israelites will worship and draw near to the Divine Presence.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Leviticus, chapter one, verse one, through chapter five, verse twenty-six. God calls out to Moses from the newly completed Tent of Meeting and begins laying out an entire system of offerings that the people of Israel will bring. First come the burnt offerings, called the olah, where an animal — a bull, a sheep, a goat, or even a bird like a turtledove or pigeon — is completely consumed on the altar as a pleasing odor to God. The person bringing the offering lays a hand on the animal's head, connecting themselves to the act, and the priests — Aaron's sons — handle the blood and the burning. Next come the grain offerings, made of choice flour mixed with oil and frankincense, which can be baked in an oven, prepared on a griddle, or cooked in a pan. A portion is burned on the altar, and the rest goes to the priests — but no leaven or honey may be burned, and every grain offering must be seasoned with salt. Then we hear about the well-being offering, a sacrifice of thanksgiving or peace, where certain fats and organs are burned but much of the animal may be shared and eaten. God gives a permanent rule: the people must never eat fat or blood. The portion then shifts to something deeply human — what happens when someone makes a mistake. God describes the purgation offering, brought when a person sins unintentionally, whether it is the high priest, the whole community, a chieftain, or an ordinary individual. Each brings a different animal depending on their status, and the priest makes expiation on their behalf so they can be forgiven. For those who cannot afford an animal, God provides alternatives — two birds, or even a simple measure of flour — ensuring that no one is shut out from the path to forgiveness. Finally, the portion addresses reparation offerings for trespasses against sacred things or dishonest dealings with others, requiring restitution of what was taken plus a fifth of its value, along with a ram brought to the priest.

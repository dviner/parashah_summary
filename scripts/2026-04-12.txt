Parashah: Tazria-Metzora (Leviticus 12:1-15:33)
Date: 2026-04-12

Welcome back to Parashah in Brief, your weekly walk through the Torah. This week we're reading a double portion called Tazria-Metzora, which continues the book of Leviticus and its detailed instructions for holiness, purity, and the sacred responsibilities of the priests.

<break time="1.5s" />

Here is our summary of Leviticus, chapter twelve, verse one, through chapter fifteen, verse thirty-three. The portion opens with laws about childbirth. When a woman bears a child, she enters a period of ritual impurity followed by a longer period of purification. At the end of that time, she brings offerings to the priest at the Tent of Meeting — a lamb and a bird, or if she cannot afford a lamb, two birds — and the priest makes atonement on her behalf. The text then shifts to something unexpected: skin diseases. When a person develops a suspicious swelling, rash, or discoloration, they must be brought before a priest for examination. The priest looks carefully at the affected skin — checking whether the hair has turned white, whether the mark goes deeper than the surface, whether it spreads over time. Depending on what the priest finds, the person may be isolated for seven days and then examined again. If the condition is confirmed as a serious scaly affliction, traditionally called leprosy, the person must tear their clothes, leave their hair uncovered, cover their upper lip, and cry out "Impure! Impure!" — living alone, outside the camp. Even garments and houses can be affected by these eruptions, and the priest inspects them too, sometimes ordering fabrics burned or stones torn from walls. But this portion is not only about diagnosis — it is also about healing and restoration. When a person recovers, an elaborate purification ritual unfolds: two live birds are brought, one is slaughtered over fresh water, and the other is dipped in the blood and set free in the open country. The recovered person shaves, washes, and brings offerings. The priest places blood and oil on their right ear, right thumb, and right big toe — symbolically restoring them to wholeness. Provision is made for those who are poor, ensuring that everyone, regardless of means, can return to community and to holiness.

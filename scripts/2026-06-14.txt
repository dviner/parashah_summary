Parashah: Korach (Numbers 16:1-18:32)
Date: 2026-06-14

Welcome to Parashah in Brief, and thanks so much for joining me this week. We're turning to Parashat Korach, a dramatic and powerful portion from the book of Numbers that plunges us right into one of the most serious rebellions the Israelites ever launched against Moses and Aaron in the wilderness.

Here is our summary of Numbers, chapter sixteen, verse one, through chapter eighteen, verse thirty-two. The portion opens with a Levite named Korach rallying two hundred and fifty community leaders, along with Dathan and Abiram from the tribe of Reuben, to confront Moses and Aaron. Their argument is bold: the entire community is holy, they say, so why should Moses and Aaron elevate themselves above everyone else? Moses, deeply shaken, proposes a test — let each man bring a fire pan with incense before God the next morning, and God will reveal whom He has truly chosen. Meanwhile, Dathan and Abiram refuse even to come when Moses summons them, accusing him of dragging the people out of Egypt only to lord over them in the desert. The next day, as the rebels gather at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, God's presence appears and threatens to destroy the entire community. Moses and Aaron plead on the people's behalf, and God relents — but only partially. Moses warns everyone to pull back from the tents of Korach, Dathan, and Abiram, and in a terrifying moment, the earth opens up and swallows the rebels and their households alive, while a divine fire consumes the two hundred and fifty men offering incense. Astonishingly, the very next day the people blame Moses and Aaron for the deaths, and a devastating plague breaks out, killing fourteen thousand seven hundred before Aaron rushes into the crowd with incense to stop it. To settle the question of leadership once and for all, God instructs each tribal chieftain to place a staff in the Tent of Meeting. The next morning, Aaron's staff has miraculously sprouted, blossomed, and produced almonds, confirming his chosen role. The portion then closes with detailed instructions about the duties of the priests and Levites, the sacred gifts and offerings they are to receive, and the tithing system that will sustain them, since they are given no land of their own — God Himself is declared to be their portion.

Parashah: Shavuot II (Deuteronomy 14:22-16:17)
Date: 2026-05-17

Welcome to Parashah in Brief! I'm so glad you're joining me this week. Today we're looking at the special reading for the second day of Shavuot, the Feast of Weeks, drawn from the book of Deuteronomy. This reading sits within Moses's great farewell address to the Israelites as they prepare to enter the Promised Land, and it lays out a remarkable vision of what a just and generous society should look like.

Here is our summary of Deuteronomy, chapter fourteen, verse twenty-two, through chapter sixteen, verse seventeen. Moses begins by commanding the people to set aside a tithe, a tenth of their harvest each year, and to consume it joyfully before God at the chosen place of worship. If the journey is too far, they may convert that tithe into money, travel to the holy place, and spend it on whatever they desire — cattle, wine, anything — and feast there with their households. But every third year, the tithe stays local, so that the Levites, the strangers, the orphans, and the widows in their communities can eat their fill. Then Moses turns to the seventh year, the year of remission, when all debts among Israelites must be forgiven. He urges the people not to harden their hearts as that year approaches, warning them never to withhold help from the needy out of selfish calculation. "Open your hand," he says, "and lend whatever is sufficient." He then addresses the freeing of Hebrew servants after six years of service, insisting they not be sent away empty-handed but furnished generously — because the Israelites themselves were once slaves in Egypt. Next, Moses instructs the people about firstborn animals, which are consecrated to God, and then he moves into the three pilgrimage festivals. He describes Passover, the Feast of Unleavened Bread, recalling the hurried departure from Egypt. He describes the Feast of Weeks, our Shavuot, celebrated seven weeks after the first harvest, with freewill offerings and communal rejoicing. And he describes the Feast of Booths, held after the autumn ingathering. In every case, the celebration must include everyone — sons, daughters, servants, Levites, strangers, orphans, and widows. Three times a year, all shall appear before God, and no one shall come empty-handed, but each according to the blessing they have received.

Parashah: Ki Tisa (Exodus 30:11-34:35)
Date: 2026-03-02

Welcome to Parashah in Brief, and thanks so much for joining me this week. We're looking at Parashat Ki Tisa, which means "when you take," and it brings us to one of the most dramatic and pivotal moments in the entire book of Exodus.

Here is our summary of Exodus, chapter thirty, verse eleven, through chapter thirty-four, verse thirty-five. The portion opens with God instructing Moses on several important matters while he is still atop Mount Sinai: every Israelite counted in a census must give a half-shekel coin as an offering, the rich and the poor giving equally. God also gives instructions for a copper washing basin for the priests, a sacred anointing oil, and a special incense — all reserved for holy use alone. God then names Bezalel and Oholiab as the master craftsmen who will build the Tabernacle, and reminds the people to keep the Sabbath, the weekly day of complete rest, as an eternal sign of the covenant. Then God gives Moses two stone tablets inscribed by God's own finger. But down below, the people grow anxious — Moses has been gone so long they fear he won't return. They pressure Aaron to make them a god, and Aaron collects their gold jewelry and casts it into a golden calf. The people bow before it and celebrate wildly. God tells Moses what has happened and threatens to destroy the entire nation, but Moses pleads passionately on their behalf, invoking the promises made to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and God relents. Moses descends the mountain, sees the calf and the revelry, and in his fury shatters the two tablets at the foot of the mountain. He burns the calf, grinds it to powder, and makes the people drink it. The tribe of Levi rallies to Moses and carries out a severe punishment. Moses then returns up the mountain to beg forgiveness for the people, even offering to be erased from God's record if they cannot be forgiven. God agrees to send the people onward but says He will not dwell in their midst. Moses pleads again, and God agrees to go with them. In one of the most intimate moments in all of scripture, God passes before Moses in a cleft of rock and proclaims the divine attributes: compassionate, gracious, slow to anger, abounding in kindness and faithfulness. Moses bows in awe, and God renews the covenant, carving new tablets to replace those that were broken.

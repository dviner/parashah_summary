Parashah: Ki Tisa (Exodus 30:11-34:35)
Date: 2026-03-02

Welcome to Parashah in Brief, and thanks so much for joining me this week. We're looking at Parashat Ki Tisa, which means "When You Take," and it brings us to one of the most dramatic and emotional moments in the entire Book of Exodus.

Here is our summary of Exodus, chapter thirty, verse eleven, through chapter thirty-four, verse thirty-five. The portion opens with God giving Moses a series of instructions while he is still up on Mount Sinai. Every Israelite counted in a census must give a half-shekel — a small silver coin — as an offering, with the rich paying no more and the poor paying no less. God then describes a special anointing oil and sacred incense for the Tabernacle, and names two gifted craftsmen, Bezalel and Oholiab, to build it all. But then the story takes a sharp turn. Moses has been on the mountain for so long that the people lose patience and beg Aaron to make them a god they can see. Aaron collects their gold jewelry, melts it down, and shapes it into a golden calf. The people celebrate before it, declaring it the god that brought them out of Egypt. Up on the mountain, God tells Moses what has happened and threatens to destroy the entire nation. Moses pleads with God, invoking the promises made to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — and God relents. But when Moses comes down and sees the calf and the wild dancing for himself, his anger erupts and he shatters the two stone tablets inscribed by God's own hand. He burns the calf, grinds it to powder, and scatters it on the water for the people to drink. The tribe of Levi rallies to Moses' side, and about three thousand people die that day. Moses then returns up the mountain to beg forgiveness on the people's behalf, even offering to be erased from God's book if they cannot be pardoned. In one of the Torah's most intimate moments, Moses asks to see God's presence, and God passes before him, proclaiming those famous words of compassion: "A God compassionate and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in kindness and faithfulness." A new covenant is made, new tablets are carved, and when Moses finally descends, his face is radiant with light — so radiant that he must wear a veil before the people.

Parashah: Ki Tisa (Exodus 30:11-34:35)
Date: 2026-03-02

Welcome back to Parashah in 5! This week we're reading Ki Tisa, which means "when you take," and it falls right in the heart of the book of Exodus. The Israelites are camped at the foot of Mount Sinai, and Moses has been up on the mountain receiving God's instructions for an extraordinarily long time.

The portion opens with God giving Moses a series of detailed commands: every Israelite counted in a census must give a half-shekel coin, rich and poor alike, as a kind of sacred contribution. God also instructs Moses on crafting a copper washing basin for the priests, a special anointing oil, and a sacred incense, all for use in the portable sanctuary. God then names two gifted artisans, Bezalel and Oholiab, to oversee the construction of the Tabernacle and all its furnishings. And just before Moses comes down the mountain, God reminds him that the people must observe Shabbat, the sabbath, as an eternal sign of their covenant. Then God hands Moses two stone tablets inscribed by God's own finger. But while Moses has been away, the people grow anxious. They pressure Aaron into collecting their gold jewelry and casting it into a golden calf, and they begin worshipping it with feasting and dancing. God tells Moses what has happened and threatens to destroy the entire nation, but Moses argues passionately on the people's behalf, invoking God's promises to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and God relents. Moses descends, sees the calf and the revelry with his own eyes, and in a burst of fury he shatters the two tablets at the base of the mountain. He burns the calf, grinds it to powder, and makes the people drink it. After a painful reckoning, Moses goes back up the mountain and pleads with God to forgive the people, even offering to be erased from God's book if they cannot be forgiven. God agrees to continue leading the people but warns that consequences will follow. In one of the most intimate moments in all of scripture, Moses asks to see God's presence, and God places him in a cleft in the rock, passing by and revealing the divine qualities of compassion, grace, and abundant kindness. Moses then carves two new tablets, and God inscribes them again, renewing the covenant with this stubborn but beloved people.
